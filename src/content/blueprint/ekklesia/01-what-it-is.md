The word *Ekklesia* comes directly from Athenian democratic practice. After the reforms of 462 BCE, the Ekklesia became the sovereign authority of the polis — not an advisory body, not a consultative forum, but the body whose decisions carried the full weight of law. Every eligible citizen was a member. Every eligible citizen had a voice.

That experiment ran for 140 years. It ended not because it failed, but because Philip of Macedon defeated Athens at Chaeronea in 338 BCE and the experiment was interrupted by force. Two millennia of representative systems, filtered republics, and managed democracies followed — each built, in one way or another, to keep ordinary people at arm's length from the decisions that govern their lives.

The 21st-century Ekklesia carries the same foundational concept forward with critical enhancements. It includes every legal citizen regardless of gender, age, or background — correcting Athens' exclusions as a matter of foundational principle. It operates at scales the Athenians could never have imagined, made possible by networked technology, AI assistance, and cybersecurity that simply didn't exist until now.

A few terms worth understanding before you go further:

**Ekklesia** — the body itself; a legal entity consisting of every eligible citizen within a defined geographic jurisdiction, existing continuously whether or not it is assembled.

**Pnyx** — the physical or virtual assembly space where the Ekklesia gathers. Named for the hillside in Athens where citizens met in open air, accountable to their neighbors.

**Domain** — the jurisdiction of an Assembly; the geographic boundary or subject-matter scope defining what an Assembly may address and who has the right to participate.

**Assembly** — a subset of the Ekklesia constituted by the citizens within a specific domain. Assemblies have genuine legislative authority within their jurisdiction.

**Session** — a specific meeting of an Assembly. You need not have attended previous sessions to vote on a **Psephisma** when it comes to the floor, though you are expected to have reviewed previous session summaries.

The Ekklesia is not a body that represents the people. It *is* the people — and its will, spoken clearly and with strong consensus, is legally and literally the will of the people.
