When a Hypothesis reaches a session of the Ekklesia, it is opened to the full Assembly for debate. Any member of the Ekklesia may speak, question, propose modifications, argue for merger with another Hypothesis, or argue against it entirely. The floor belongs to the demos.

What makes deliberation in AreTéCracy different from any parliamentary or congressional body we know of is more than its rules of conduct or the assistance of IsoKratÓS to help with real-time monitoring. What makes it unique are the principles that the monitoring and rules protect. Every person has a right to be heard, to be served by the Ekklesia, and to be treated with dignity and respect. All discourse in an Assembly must honor those principles without fail.

This is keenly evident when one explores the rules themselves. The most important one is steelmanning. Before any citizen may argue against a position, they must demonstrate they understand the strongest possible version of the argument for it. They do this by restating the proponent's argument in their own words, to the proponent's satisfaction. Only once that is confirmed may they proceed with their critique. This rule does not prevent diverse opinions; it structures disagreement so that any criticism is about the idea, not a distortion of it.

IsoKratÓS monitors the floor in real time. Logical fallacies, manipulative rhetoric, and weak citations are flagged to Coordinators, who bring them to the Chairman. Speakers are expected to observe the proper decorum and to be honest and forthright in their statements. When necessary, their arguments are held to account in front of the entire Assembly the moment a problem is identified. The incentive to argue honestly is structural, not just ethical.

Debate is not a performance in AreTéCracy, it is a tool for finding common ground. It is not a contest of who can land the best talking points or "own" an opponent. Its purpose is to arrive at a collective judgment about whether the Hypothesis serves the polis — what needs clarification, what objections require answers, what changes might make it stronger.

The Ekklesia's goal at the end of deliberation is a Homonoic vote, meaning a strong consensus, not merely a simple majority. It is AreTéCracy's position that any law passed by 51% of the vote, or anything close to that, is inherently a ***bad law*** that only divides the polis, not serves it. If Homonoia over the Hypothesis' purpose is achieved, it advances to the Aegis. If not, the Assembly decides together what happens next — refine it, merge it, pilot it, or set it aside. Nothing dies without intention.
