*Psephisma* comes from the Greek word for pebble — a reference to the stones Athenian citizens once dropped into urns to cast their votes. The physical act of each citizen making a choice, one at a time, is built into the word itself.

When the Aegis returns the armored Psephisma to the Ekklesia, the Assembly votes. This is not a simple majority vote. The standard is Homonoia: genuine broad agreement, a consensus strong enough that those who didn't vote for it can still live under it without feeling their interests were simply outvoted.

A law that achieves Homonoia is durable. It reflects something close to the true "will of the people." It is neither a negotiated capitulation between factions nor a forced compromise nobody really likes. It is an agreement that enough citizens, deliberating honestly, found workable and fair.

When Homonoia is achieved, the Psephisma becomes Nomos, or canon law. It enters the *pandektai*, or corpus juris of the polis. The citizens who proposed it, debated it, and voted it forward did this; not a representative acting on their behalf. There was no secretive party caucus deciding in a closed session. The people decide for themselves; directly and in the open.

That is what makes the word matter. *Nomos* in ancient Greek was not just "a rule." It was the expression of the community's agreement about how it wanted to live together. AreTéCracy takes that meaning seriously. A Nomos enacted through this process carries a legitimacy that top-down legislation rarely achieves: it was built in public, by the people it governs, with every dissenting voice heard and answered before the final vote was called.

The pebble drops and the law is made by the very people it will govern. That is the real reflection of democracy.
