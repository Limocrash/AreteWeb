In a representative government, when a bill fails to pass, it is typically considered "dead." It may be reintroduced in a future session, but the system provides no structured path for refinement, no mechanism for the losing side to engage constructively with the winning side's objections, and no incentive to find a version that more people can live with. The incentive, if anything, runs the other way: keep the issue alive as a campaign weapon.

AreTéCracy treats a failed Homonoia vote differently. When the Ekklesia votes on a Psephisma and does not reach Homonoia, the question is not "do we discard this?" but "there are people who do want this. What does the polis need to be able to say yes?"

Several paths exist, and the Ekklesia chooses which to pursue based on where the disagreement actually lies. The following are some typical examples of what might be considered:

**Revision and compromise.** If the objections are specific and answerable, the Hypothesis returns to deliberation with the objections named and the proponents invited to address them. Sometimes the gap is smaller than the vote suggests.

**Geographic or domain pilot.** If there is genuine uncertainty about how a policy will work in practice, a limited trial in a specific municipality, region, or sector where the Hypothesis has potential can generate real evidence for wider acceptance, or two alternative versions of the concept might be trialed. This is what development engineers call "A/B testing" redefined as a civic process.

**Splitting.** Sometimes a Hypothesis carries two distinct ideas: one broadly acceptable, one genuinely contested. Provided the Proponent(s) agree, separating them might allow the consensus part to move forward while the contested part gets the additional deliberation it needs.

**Feeding the multitude.** By combining measures that serve different interests, a compromise can be reached that nearly everyone is happy with. This is a logical approach to issues like infrastructure that serve one particular area of the polis; simply combine projects for multiple areas so everyone receives something.

**Referral to a different level.** If the disagreement reveals that the issue is more or less localized than the submitting Ekklesia's scope, referral to the appropriate level can resolve the mismatch.

**Setting aside.** If the Assembly reaches genuine consensus that the idea isn't workable in any form, it can be formally closed — with the record noting what was tried, what objections prevailed, and what would need to change for it to be revisited. A formally closed Hypothesis may always be resubmitted; conditions change, and so can consensus.

There are no rigid limits on what an Ekklesia can do with a Hypothesis that hasn't yet found its consensus. What is not permitted is the thing modern politics does by default: letting it sit unresolved indefinitely, cycling it back as a campaign weapon season after season. The goal is always the same — Homonoia, eventually, on something the polis can actually live with.
