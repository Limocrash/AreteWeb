The citizen assembly — the Ekklesia — receives the Brillianteer Pool and performs its single, precisely bounded function in this pipeline: it reviews the qualifications of each candidate and, through Homonoia (consensus), approves a final pool. This final list is called the masterstone pool.

The term comes from the diamond trade, where a masterstone is the reference gem used to grade all others — the established standard of comparison. The masterstone pool is the group about whom the Ekklesia has collectively agreed: any one of these individuals is qualified to hold this position. It is a statement of general confidence, not a ranking.

By the time the masterstone pool is finalized, every candidate in it is, to carry the metaphor forward, effectively "inclusion-free" — the flaws and disqualifying factors have been removed at earlier stages, leaving only candidates whose quality has been confirmed at every level of the process.

The Ekklesia's power here is real and significant: it sets the standard. But it does not pick the person. That boundary is not a limitation on democratic power; it is the mechanism that keeps democratic power from being captured by faction, charisma, or manipulation.

The assembly agrees on excellence. What happens next is beyond politics entirely.
