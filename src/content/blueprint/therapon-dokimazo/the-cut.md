*Dokimazo* is a Greek verb: to test, to prove, to temper. Athens called their candidate scrutiny *dokimazía* because their metaphor was metallurgical — heating, pounding, and bending material to determine whether it could bear weight. AreTéCracy adopts the name for the same reason it adopts so much Hellenic vocabulary: to signal genuine continuity with the democratic tradition Athens began.

But the metaphor changes here, deliberately. Athenian dokimazía was a post-selection audit — a final check on someone already chosen. AreTéCracy inverts the sequence entirely. Examination happens *before* selection, which means we are not testing a commitment already made. We are finding the gems before we need them — so that whenever the kleroterion eventually draws a name, qualification has already been established at every level of the pipeline.

That is a gemologist's work, not a blacksmith's. And the rest of this map speaks that language accordingly.

The Dokimazo Corps — rotating citizen examiners, themselves sortition-selected and term-limited — conducts that search. Competence, character, domain expertise, track record, and the harder qualities that predict whether someone holds under pressure when no one is watching. IsoKratÓS assists here more than at any other node: cross-referencing records, surfacing patterns, flagging what human reviewers might miss or be socially reluctant to name.

All evaluation is conducted anonymously. A citizen is evaluated first as a set of qualifications — their Pinakion — before personal identity enters the process at any stage. This is not an incidental technical detail. It is a foundational privacy protection and a foundational bias protection built into the architecture of the system.

What leaves this node is not a finished thing. It is material the system has confirmed is worth continuing to work.
