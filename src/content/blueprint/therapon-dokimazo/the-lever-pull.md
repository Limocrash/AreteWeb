The Kleroterion was Athens's allotment machine — a stone slab with columns of slots for name-tokens, a randomization tube, and a supply of black and white balls. Its function was radical and deliberate: to take human choice out of human appointment. If chance makes the selection, there is no one to bribe, no coalition to build, no rhetoric to deploy. The machine does not have ambitions. It cannot be flattered.

AreTéCracy's Kleroterion operates on the same principle, updated for scale. The Masterstone Pool — every member confirmed qualified by the Ekklesia — enters the kleroterion. One name emerges. Not the most famous, not the most connected, not the one who wanted it most visibly. The one chance selected from among those excellence confirmed.

Why sortition? The modern electoral process selects from a pool defined almost entirely by money, performance, and the ability to make promises there's no structural obligation to keep. The qualities that win elections — charisma, stamina, the ability to perform under cameras for months — have almost nothing to do with the qualities that make someone an effective servant of the polis.

By contrast, every name in the Masterstone Pool has passed through the Dokimazo Corps — anonymously evaluated, bias removed, record confirmed. Every name has been brillianteered. Every name has been approved as fully qualified by the Ekklesia by far more than a simple majority. By the time the kleroterion draws, three independent layers of quality confirmation have already done their work.

If you want to call sortition a keno wheel, consider this: it's a keno wheel where there are no losing slots. Whatever name is chosen, the polis wins.

Random selection is not a weakness. It is the feature that closes the last remaining door to corruption and factioning. The mechanism has no ambitions, nor can it be flattered. And unlike every human selector in the history of governance, it has no favorites.

You cannot be more persuasive than chance.
