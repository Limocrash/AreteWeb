Something fundamental is shifting in the relationship between humans and work. Automation and artificial intelligence are steadily removing the economic necessity of human labor from one industry after another. AreTéCracy has an answer to what comes next: there is one job that cannot be automated, that AI cannot perform on its own, and that humans will never be comfortable delegating entirely to machines — governing themselves. Self-governance is the one irreducibly human occupation. Under AreTéCracy, it is also your civic employment.

Citizenship in AreTéCracy is not a passive legal status. It is an active relationship between the individual and the polis — one that comes with genuine rights, genuine responsibilities, and genuine compensation.

The pipeline begins with a voluntary act. A citizen decides they wish to serve the polis in a Therapon role and steps forward as a candidate. This is not a job application, and it is emphatically not a political campaign. There is no constituency to build, no opponent to defeat, no donor to court. It is a declaration of intent and a willingness to be examined — the beginning of a relationship with public service grounded entirely in merit and civic duty.

No one is conscripted. No one is shamed into compliance. But the choice not to participate is not consequence-free. What you never lose is your Isotimia — equal standing as a citizen — or any of the basic rights it confers. Your vote counts one for one, identical to every other citizen's, regardless of your participation level.

The cost of not participating is twofold. First, your Axia — the civic merit record that shapes your civic reputation — will honestly reflect your record. Second, your access to government-provided benefit tiers will adjust on a sliding scale.

Plato wrote about this in *Republic* 347c: when good, qualified people refuse to govern, they suffer the consequence of being governed by those less good or less qualified than themselves. For us it is not a moral argument; it is a structural observation about what fills a vacuum. We call it Plato's Roll Call.

What enters the pipeline at this node is not a supplicant nor a politician. It is a citizen who has chosen to show up for the one job that cannot be outsourced — who has declared their readiness to be examined, evaluated honestly, and placed in a pool from which chance and the assembly may one day draw their name. The offer in the open hand is not ambition. It is something rarer and more demanding: civic availability.
