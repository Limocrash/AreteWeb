The Dokimazo process ends with a citizen standing before the Ekklesia and placing their hand on the symbolic scroll of their mandate. This is the same assembly that confirmed their qualification, approved them as eligible to be in the Masterstone Pool, and wrote the mandate they are about to accept.

The Athenians understood the weight of this moment. Their oath of office — the Ephebic Oath, taken by young citizens entering civic life — was a public commitment before the community, witnessed and binding. AreTéCracy carries that tradition forward.

For higher, magistrate-level Therapons, there will be an apprenticeship lasting an entire term: working in the position alongside a mentor whom they will replace in the following term, then becoming a tutor to the next apprentice who will replace them. For lower Therapon levels there will be an onboarding and training process appropriate to the role. There will be regular reporting to the Ekklesia on their progress, and Axia tracking that serves as both insight and, if needed, probative evidence in a formal Peira proceeding.

In AreTéCracy, Therapons have a duty to see their jobs as servants of the polis, and to treat every citizen they encounter with honesty, care, and *Philoxenia* — the hospitality that the Greeks have never completely forgotten, the virtue that says every stranger may be someone worth treating with the full grace of a host.

The specific form the swearing-in takes belongs to each Ekklesia to determine according to its customs. What is not optional is the act itself: the public acceptance of the mandate, before the people, on the record.

When the hand lifts from the scroll, the Therapon's service has begun. The authority they carry is real, bounded, and borrowed from the Ekklesia. And when the term ends, they return to the civilian economy — or back to the Registry, if they choose — carrying deeper experience and higher Axia, confirmed not once but twice by the polis.
