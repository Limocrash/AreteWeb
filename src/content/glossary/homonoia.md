##### *Like-Mindedness / Strong Consensus*

*From Greek: homos (ὁμός, same) + nous (νοῦς, mind) = "like-mindedness"*

Strong consensus principle requiring broad agreement (minimum 70%) before major decisions become law. Not unanimity—which paralyzes governance—but sufficient agreement to prevent factional tyranny.

**Athens didn't practice this.** They used simple majority rule. Homonoia was an aspiration, not their standard. **It only took 280 out of ~501 votes, or about 56%, to kill Socrates.** And killing Socrates for the capital crime of exercising free speach and annoying elites pissed off Plato, making tyranny of the majority the single largest criticism of the democracy model for the next 2,000 years. AreTéCracy has learned from that failure: If you're within a statistical margin of error, you might as well flip a coin, and the coin is probably more objective than you are. Our motto: "**NEVER** kill Socrates. Just get Homonoia."

Higher thresholds (80%, 90%, 95%) apply to increasingly foundational matters. This prevents both majoritarianism (51% tyranny) and paralysis (unanimity gridlock).

The barrier to consensus isn't the threshold—it's learning to deliberate as equals rather than factions. Supported by IsoKratOS (structured deliberation), Axia (elevating competence), and iterative revision.
