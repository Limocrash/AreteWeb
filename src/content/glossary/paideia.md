##### *Education* *as Duty*

*From paideia (παιδεία), meaning holistic upbringing, education, and cultural formation.*

For the Greeks it was never just “school”; it was the whole process by which a person became fit to live as a citizen among other citizens—ethically, intellectually, and aesthetically.

In AreTéCracy, Paideia is the lifelong project of forming people who can actually live in Areté. It includes critical thinking, media literacy, basic economics, history, rhetoric, philosophy, and the Aretai:

* Sonder and Philotimo — developing the habit of seeing other people as real and wanting to help them,

* PRC — the moral duty to serve democracy in order to protect it, and

* Alētheia — to be transparent and honest in all dealings.

Paideia is not confined to formal schooling. It runs through public media, cultural institutions, civic games and simulations, and the way families and communities are supported in raising children who can one day sit in the Ekklesia without being manipulated by demagogues or algorithms.

A key expression of Paideia in AreTéCracy is the **Junior Ekklesia**: a parallel set of Assemblies for younger citizens mirroring the structures of the adult Ekklesia in age‑appropriate form. There, children and adolescents learn how to deliberate, disagree, seek Homonoia, and see the consequences of policy choices in simulated or low‑stakes environments. Junior deliberations do not directly bind adult law, but their outcomes and perspectives are relayed to the main Ekklesia and can inform debate, especially on long‑horizon issues like climate, education, and generational fairness.
