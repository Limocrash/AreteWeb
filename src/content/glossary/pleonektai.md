##### *Oligarchs and Would-be Oligarchs*

*From Greek pleonexia (πλεονεξία), greed; the drive to take more than one’s fair share*

Those who try to take more than their share—of wealth, power, status, or control. In Greek political memory, pleonektai were not just “greedy people,” but the ones who repeatedly turned themselves into despots. When someone hamartanei—“misses the mark” of Areté—kakia (moral corruption) would follow, and pleonexia was never far behind. The Athenians learned the hard way that if you let such people accumulate unchecked leverage, you don’t just get inequality; you get tyranny.

In AreTéCracy, Pleonektai are the natural adversaries of Isotimía and therefore democracy itself. We assume they will appear—among wealthy individuals, corporate structures, media barons, or office-holders drifting toward “I am the state.” The system is designed with that tendency in mind. Short terms, sortition, Dokimazo, Euthynia, constraints on Survival Stack ownership, the Workshop Economy, Logodosia, Axia decay, and modernized Ostrakismos all exist largely because pleonexia is one of the key reasons democracies fail.

The point is not to pretend we can abolish pleonexia. It is to make sure pleonektai cannot quietly turn the polis into their private loot bag.
