_Coined by John Koenig in his excellent 'Dictionary of Obscure Sorrows; his inspiration: German *sonder-* ("special") and French *sonder* ("to probe")._ ***We're borrowing it because we know of no better term in any language***

The third of the Four Aretai. The sudden, visceral recognition that every person you pass has a life as vivid, complex, and real as your own—with their own ambitions, frustrations, relationships, and inner world just as intricate as yours.

Koenig describes it as "the idea that we all think of ourselves as the main character in our own story, and everyone else as just extras. But in reality, we're all the main character, and you yourself are an extra in someone else's story."

**Philosophically:** The experiential side of intersubjective reality.

**Why this matters for Isotimía:** You can't genuinely grant equal standing to people you experience as NPCs or extras in your story. But once you see their existence as being just as valid as your own, with needs and concerns as valid as yours, you realize you must give them equal standing as human beings. It's what prevents the "othering," or ignoring the Isotímia of others from doing the same to you.

When Sonder becomes a reflex, you start treating everyone as if their standing actually matters—because you can't unfeel their reality once you've truly recognized it. And that's when Isotimía becomes more than just a wise legal principle.
