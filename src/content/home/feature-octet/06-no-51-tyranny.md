It is our contention that simple majorities exist for reasons that have nothing to do with serving the "will of the people." Rather, they are a tool used by representative governments to generate divisiveness, factionism, and hate for the purpose of raising campaign funds and little else.

Real democracy, however, is people working together to find solutions that serve as much of the polis (people) as it possibly can. If that goal is achieved, laws should earn supermajorities or even hypermajorities of consensus. Consider this as the antithesis of slim margins that leave fully half the people seething with anger and doing pogos, waiting for their faction to grow by 2 or 3 percent so they can jump into the ring and deliver equal pain to the other side.

Explaining the details of why a so-called majority is ***truly*** anything but the "will of the people" is complex, so to find out more we suggest reading our more complete dissertation on the subject in the [Ekklesia](/blueprint/ekklesia) hub of our Blueprint section.

The bottom line, however, is that we believe any law which half the population hates is, by definition, a ***bad law***. We require *Homonoia* (Like-mindedness) before any proposal can become canon law. We're talking at least 60%, and after an Ekklesia acquires a little practice, more than 70% agreement on any proposal that passes. If a proposal can't earn broad agreement, it goes back to the workshop. We don't move forward until we move forward together. That is the trademark of a polis that cares about all of its people.
